# Copy Frameworks Reference

Cold email copy frameworks, personalization strategies, and sequence architecture. Come here for detailed patterns when writing campaigns.

---

## The SPARK Framework

Every cold email follows this structure:

| Element | What It Does | Length |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| **S** — Subject | Pattern interrupt. Lowercase, 3-5 words, no clickbait. | 3-5 words |
| **P** — Personalized Opening | Prove homework. Reference a signal. | 1-2 lines |
| **A** — Agitate the Problem | Name the pain in their language, not yours. | 2-3 lines |
| **R** — Relevant Value | One concrete outcome with a number from a similar company. | 2-3 lines |
| **K** — Kick-off CTA | Low-friction question. Not "book a call." | 1 line |

**Total: 60-120 words. Never exceed 150.**

## 6 Copy Archetypes

### 1. The Observation
Best for: companies showing a visible signal (hiring, funding, product launch)

```
- Line 1: What you noticed (the signal)
- Line 2-3: What that usually means (the implication)
- Line 4-5: What others in their position did (social proof)
- CTA: "Seeing the same thing on your end?"
```
Works when: the signal is specific and timely. Fails when: the observation is generic ("I see you're growing!").

### 2. The Problem/Solution
Best for: well-understood pain points with measurable impact

```
- Line 1: Name the specific problem
- Line 2: Quantify the cost
- Line 3-4: How you solve it (outcome, not features)
- Line 5: Proof point from similar company
- CTA: "Worth a quick look?"
```
Works when: the problem is real and actively felt. Fails when: the problem is assumed.

### 3. The Referral Ceiling
Best for: founder-led businesses relying on word-of-mouth

```
- Line 1: Acknowledge their growth channel (referrals, inbound)
- Line 2: Name the limitation ("can't control when the next one comes")
- Line 3: Contrast with predictable alternative
- Line 4: Brief credential
- CTA: "Would it help to see the playbook?"
```
Works when: targeting founders who know they need outbound but haven't started.

### 4. The Creative Ideas
Best for: prospects where you can generate specific campaign ideas

```
- Line 1: "Our team analyzed {{companyName}} and came up with 3 ideas"
- Lines 2-4: Three specific, tailored concepts
- CTA: "Think any of these could work?"
```
Works when: ideas are genuinely specific. Fails when: they could apply to any company.

### 5. The Benchmark
Best for: data-driven buyers (VPs, Directors)

```
- Line 1: Industry benchmark or data point
- Line 2: How their performance likely compares
- Line 3: What top performers do differently
- CTA: "Want to see how you stack up?"
```
Works when: you have real benchmark data.

### 6. The Whole Offer
Best for: strong value prop when no specific signal exists

```
- Line 1: One-sentence credibility
- Line 2-3: Specific result with proof
- Line 4: Relevance bridge to their company
- CTA: "Open to hearing more?"
```
Use this as the default when you can't find a signal.

## Pattern Breaking

Most cold emails follow the same tired skeleton: compliment or observation → pitch → "book a call?" Recipients can smell this structure in the first 3 words. The goal is to make your email feel like it came from a real person with a real thought, not a sequence tool.

### Why Patterns Kill Replies

A cleaning company owner getting 10+ cold emails a week has trained their brain to recognize and delete them. They don't even read past the first line. Your email has to break that reflex. Not with gimmicks, but by genuinely sounding different.

### Pattern-Breaking Openers

Instead of "I noticed your company..." or "Congrats on...", try:

| Technique | Example | Why It Works |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **The Blunt Question** | "how are you getting new clients right now?" | Feels like a real conversation, not a pitch |
| **The Counterintuitive** | "most marketing agencies will tell you to run ads. for a 10-person cleaning company, that's probably wrong." | Challenges expectations, creates curiosity |
| **The Neighbor** | "we just helped [specific company in their area] — figured I'd reach out while I'm in the neighborhood" | Hyper-local, feels personal |
| **The Admission** | "this is a cold email. I won't pretend it isn't. but I think you'll want to read the next 2 lines." | Disarming honesty |
| **The Micro-Story** | "a landscaper in Fresno said he was turning down jobs because he couldn't hire fast enough" | Story pulls them in before they realize it's outreach |
| **The Confused** | "I was looking at your Google reviews (4.8 stars, 200+ reviews) and genuinely curious why you're not showing up in more searches" | Genuine question based on research |

### "Poke the Bear" Questions

Instead of pitching your solution, ask a **neutral question** that makes them rethink their current one. Not a leading question. A genuinely curious one.

Bad: "If I could show you how to get more leads, would you be interested?"
Good: "How do you know your current marketing setup is actually bringing in the right customers?"

More examples:
- "How do you know your current website isn't turning away the exact customers you want?"
- "If you're relying on referrals, how do you know what happens in the months when nobody refers?"
- "How do you know the leads you're getting from Google are actually worth what you're paying?"

The shift: **question their status quo** instead of selling your replacement. Let them arrive at the gap themselves.

### "So What?" Messaging

Take your commoditized offer and push through to the **second-order effect**. Most cold emails stop at the service. Go one level deeper.

- "We do digital marketing" → **so what?** → "We fill your schedule with booked jobs so you stop worrying about where next month's revenue is coming from."
- "We build automations" → **so what?** → "We build automations so you can stop hiring admin staff and put that money back into the business."
- "We manage your online presence" → **so what?** → "We manage your online presence so when someone Googles 'plumber near me,' you're the one they call, not your competitor."

Every sentence in your email should survive the "so what?" test. If the prospect wouldn't care, cut it.

### Problem Sniffing

Use data scraping to find an **actual, specific problem** the prospect has, then reference it directly. This is the highest-impact personalization technique.

Examples:
- Scrape their Google reviews, find a negative one, reference it: "I noticed a 2-star review from last month mentioning wait times. That kind of thing can tank your local ranking."
- Check their website load speed, reference it: "Your site took 8 seconds to load on mobile. That's costing you about 40% of visitors."
- Check their Google Business Profile for missing info, outdated hours, no posts.

This works because it proves you actually looked at their business, and it names a problem they can verify in 30 seconds.

### Rules for Pattern Breaking

1. **Never open with their name + company observation.** That's the #1 cold email tell. If you must personalize the opener, weave it into a question or story.
2. **First line should be readable without context.** If someone saw just the preview text, they should want to open it.
3. **Vary email structure across the sequence.** If Step 1 opens with a question, Step 2 should open with a story or data point. Don't repeat the same pattern.
4. **Short beats long for Step 1.** A 40-60 word first email with a single genuine question outperforms a 120-word pitch. Save the detail for follow-ups. A 2-sentence email can outperform a highly researched one.
5. **Read it out loud.** If it sounds like something you'd say to someone at a BBQ, it's good. If it sounds like a marketing email, rewrite it.
6. **Never mention "cold email" when prompting AI to write copy.** AI is trained on bad cold email advice from the internet. Instead say "help me write the first sentence of a persuasive message."

## Subject Line Formulas

| Formula | Example | When |
|---------|---------|------|
| `{{companyName}} + [topic]` | `acme + outbound` | Default |
| `[mutual connection] recommended` | `sarah recommended I reach out` | Real referral only |
| `[role] question` | `quick vp sales question` | Targeting specific titles |
| `[trigger event]` | `re: your series B` | Signal-based |
| `[metric] idea` | `pipeline idea` | Metric-driven prospects |
| `[competitor] alternative` | `thinking beyond zoominfo` | Competitive displacement |

**Rules:**
- Always lowercase
- No brackets, exclamation marks, or emojis
- 3-5 words max
- No "Re:" or "Fwd:" tricks (destroys trust)
- Test in batches of 200+ before drawing conclusions

## Personalization Hierarchy

| Level | Type | Example | Effort | Impact |
|-------|------|---------|--------|--------|
| **5** | Behavioral Signal | "Saw you posted about switching from HubSpot" | High | Highest |
| **4** | Business Event | "Congrats on the Series B" | Medium-High | Very High |
| **3** | Company-Specific | "Hiring 3 SDRs — building the outbound team?" | Medium | High |
| **2** | Role-Specific | "Most VPs of Sales at Series B companies..." | Low-Med | Medium |
| **1** | Industry-Specific | "SaaS companies in your space typically..." | Low | Low-Med |
| **0** | None | "Hi, I wanted to reach out..." | None | Lowest |

### The Personalization Formula

```
[OBSERVATION] + [IMPLICATION] + [BRIDGE]
```

- **Observation:** what you noticed (the signal)
- **Implication:** what that usually means
- **Bridge:** how it connects to your value

Bad: "Hi Sarah, I see you work at Acme Corp."
Good: "Sarah — noticed Acme just posted 3 SDR roles in Austin. Scaling the outbound team usually means the founder-led phase worked, but the playbook isn't documented yet."

The difference: bad personalization names facts. Good personalization draws insights from facts.

### Bucket Personalization (for 1,000+ leads)

Segment the list by signal type. Write bucket-specific openers:

| Bucket | Opening Line |
|--------|-------------|
| Recently funded | "Post-{{fundingRound}}, most {{industry}} companies realize outbound needs to be a machine, not a side project." |
| Hiring sales | "Hiring {{openRoles}} usually means founder-led sales worked — now you need the playbook." |
| Tech stack change | "Teams switching to {{newTool}} are usually 90 days into a bigger GTM overhaul." |
| Industry pain | "{{industry}} companies at your stage typically hit a wall at {{milestone}}." |
| No signal | Use Archetype 6 (Whole Offer) — lead with strongest proof point |

Layer in auto-populated variables (name, company) for the personal touch.

## 4-Step Sequence Architecture (Two-Thread Model)

Structure the sequence as **two pairs of two emails in separate threads**, not one long chain. Threading all follow-ups in one thread reminds them they already ignored you. A fresh thread on Email 3 gets a "new email" response rate.

| Step | Day | Thread | Goal | Angle |
|------|-----|--------|------|-------|
| **1** | 0 | Thread A | Reply | Strongest hook. Poke the bear or pattern-breaking opener. |
| **2** | 3-4 | Thread A (reply) | Reply | Add depth: case study details, proof point, AI-personalized line |
| **3** | 7-8 | Thread B (new subject) | Reply | Different value angle. Lower friction CTA. |
| **4** | 14 | Thread B (reply) | Redirect | Referral ask. Name a specific person at their company. |

**Total: 14 days, 4 emails, 2 threads.**

### Email-by-Email Purpose

**Email 1:** Lead with research/homework. Answer "why you, why now?" Use a pattern-breaking opener. Keep it under 75 words.

**Email 2:** Thread reply to Email 1. Add the context you cut from Email 1 to keep it short. Expand on the case study. Can include an AI-personalized line.

**Email 3:** New thread, new subject line. Switch the value angle entirely (e.g., if Email 1 was "save time," Email 3 is "stop losing money"). Lower the friction: "if I made a quick breakdown of how this would work for {{companyName}}, would you look at it?"

**Email 4:** Thread reply to Email 3. NOT a breakup email. A referral redirect: "I know there are about {{employee_count}} people at {{companyName}} and maybe this isn't your call. Should I reach out to {{specific_name}} instead given their role?" Using a real name from their team creates urgency.

### Extended Multi-Channel (high-value targets)

| Step | Day | Channel | Objective |
|------|-----|---------|-----------|
| 1 | 0 | Email | Signal-based opening |
| 2 | 1 | LinkedIn | Connection + personalized note |
| 3 | 3 | Email | New value angle |
| 4 | 5 | LinkedIn | Engage with their content |
| 5 | 7 | Email | Case study or social proof |
| 6 | 10 | LinkedIn | DM with resource |
| 7 | 14 | Email | Breakup or referral |

### Variant Testing

- 2-3 variants per step
- Only test ONE variable per variant pair
- Minimum 200 sends per variant
- Winner = highest **positive** reply rate (not total)

### Key Sequence Rules

- Each follow-up adds NEW information (never "just bumping this")
- Step 2 should be a reply to Step 1 (same thread, higher opens)
- Write backwards: breakup email first, then work back to Step 1
- If open rate drops below 40% mid-campaign, stop. Deliverability problem.

## Objection Pre-Handling in Sequences

Layer objection handling into Steps 2-3:

| Step | Pre-Handle |
|------|-----------|
| 1 | Opening email (earn the reply) |
| 2 | "Does this work?" — send a case study |
| 3 | "We already have a solution" — show the gap |
| 4 | "Not now" — create urgency or low-commitment next step |

### AER Framework (for live objection handling)

**A — Acknowledge:** "Totally understand — [validate their concern]."
**E — Explore:** "Curious — [question to understand the real issue]?"
**R — Redirect:** "[New perspective or data that addresses the concern]."

## Copy QA Checklist

Before any email goes live:

- [ ] Subject: 3-5 words, lowercase, no clickbait
- [ ] First line: references something specific (not company size or industry)
- [ ] Length: under 120 words (150 max)
- [ ] Reading level: grade 5-7
- [ ] CTA: one question, low-friction
- [ ] Tone: would you say this in a real conversation?
- [ ] "So what" test: every sentence — if the prospect wouldn't care, cut it
- [ ] Variables: all {{variables}} resolve for every lead
- [ ] Mobile preview: no long paragraphs or text walls
- [ ] Spam check: no trigger words (free, guarantee, act now, limited time)
- [ ] None of these phrases appear: "Hope this email finds you well," "Hi I'm [name] and I'm with [company]," "I know [titles] like you have problems with..."
- [ ] No corporate jargon: incentivize, comprehensive, ROI, streamline, engage, leverage, synergy
- [ ] The "so what?" test: push past the service to the second-order outcome ("we do marketing" → "so you stop worrying about where next month's revenue comes from")

## Metrics That Matter

| Metric | Target | What It Tells You |
|--------|--------|------------------|
| Open rate | 60-80% | Subject line + deliverability |
| Reply rate | 5-15% | Copy relevance + offer |
| Positive reply rate | 2-8% | True message-market fit |
| Bounce rate | < 3% | List quality |
| Unsubscribe | < 0.5% | Targeting accuracy |
